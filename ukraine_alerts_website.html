Forecast Model

A simple trend + seasonality model projects future alert patterns. This is a statistical estimate — actual alerts depend on military and political developments that no model can predict. Treat this as a baseline scenario only.

How this forecast works: We use a linear trend fitted on monthly totals, combined with a 12-month seasonal adjustment calculated from historical averages. The model captures the general upward trend seen since late 2023 and the tendency for alerts to peak in autumn months. Confidence intervals widen over time to reflect increasing uncertainty.

Model assumptions & limitations

• The model assumes the conflict continues at roughly current intensity.

• Ceasefire, escalation, or changes in weapons systems would significantly alter actual numbers.

• Seasonal patterns observed in 2022–2025 are assumed to continue.

• This is a simple statistical model, not a military intelligence assessment.

Do not use this forecast for safety planning or official purposes.

Methodology & Data Sources

How this analysis was built — written for a general audience with no technical background.

📡 Where does the data come from?

Ukraine maintains an official air alert system run through the Ministry of Defense. When a threat is detected — typically an incoming missile or drone — a siren sounds and a digital alert is issued for the affected region (oblast). These alerts are tracked in near real-time by alerts.in.ua, which provides a public API. The dataset compiled by Vadimkin on GitHub collects this data daily and makes it freely available for research. The data we use covers official oblast-level alerts only — city-level OSINT alerts are excluded to avoid double counting.

📊 What counts as one "alert"?

One alert = one continuous siren period in one region. An alert begins when the threat siren sounds and ends when the all-clear is given. If the same region gets two separate waves of alerts in one day, that counts as two alerts. Alerts that started but never received an official all-clear have their end time estimated (typically capped at 3 hours). Note: From Feb 24 to Mar 15, 2022, data was collected from unofficial sources and may have minor inaccuracies.

🗺️ How are regions defined?

Ukraine has 24 oblasts (regions) plus the city of Kyiv, which is its own administrative unit. Crimea is also listed in some datasets as a region but has had no official alert data since Russian annexation in 2014. Occupied parts of Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson oblasts have limited coverage — alerts apply to the Ukrainian-controlled portions.

📈 How is the forecast calculated?

We use a two-part model: (1) Trend: a straight-line fit on monthly totals shows whether alerts are generally increasing or decreasing over time. (2) Seasonality: we calculate the average deviation for each calendar month (e.g., October tends to be higher than June). The forecast combines these two components. The shaded confidence band widens over time because uncertainty grows the further into the future we project. This is a simplified model — professional forecasting would also incorporate geopolitical variables.

⚠️ Important caveats

• Alert counts are a proxy for threat intensity — they reflect how many times warnings were issued, not how many actual missiles or drones reached their targets.
• A single mass attack across all 25 regions on one day can create a large spike that looks similar to weeks of moderate activity.
• The data represents real events that affected real people. These numbers represent over 4 years of daily danger for Ukrainian civilians.
• Statistical values (alert counts, dates) in this dashboard are closely based on published research and publicly available data, but exact figures may vary slightly from primary sources.
